mysage.chat

Sage

A cancer companion you talk to like a friend, that quietly becomes an expert on your situation.

Product flow and wireframe walkthrough · v1 · July 2026 · Wondrlink / Fwollo

Overview

Sage is a companion for cancer patients and their caregivers. It records and summarizes doctor visits, explains reports and medical terms in plain words, keeps a health log, and matches patients to clinical trials, all through a single chat interface.

Sage learns about the patient gradually, through conversation, instead of demanding a long intake form. A patient gets from the front door to a live conversation in under sixty seconds, and the app earns each piece of information at the moment it becomes useful.

Product principles

Five rules every screen follows

Just-in-time, never twice

Never ask for information until a task needs it, and never ask for the same thing twice. The profile is a byproduct of using the app, not a gate in front of it. Every profile field is nullable, and every feature degrades gracefully when a field is missing.

Sixth-grade words, doctor-grade data

Patients answer in their own words ("it spread to my liver"). Sage translates to clinical vocabulary (metastatic, hepatic involvement) behind the scenes, and shows both languages back to the patient so they can verify and learn.

The chat is the app

Every feature is an entry point into the same conversation. Buttons don't open forms; they inject an opening line into the chat, and Sage collects what it's missing conversationally.

Patient-provided only

In v1, Sage knows only what the patient shares: typed, spoken, or uploaded. No provider integrations, no PHI authorizations. The UI says so plainly: "We only know what you share."

Escalate, don't reassure

Sage never diagnoses and never talks a worried patient out of seeking care. Urgent symptoms trigger an immediate, unambiguous handoff to the care team or 911, followed by one useful non-medical action.

The flow at a glance

One journey, five phases

The whole product is a single path with one branch: is the account holder the patient, or a caregiver? That choice, made once during onboarding, changes the voice of every screen after it, but not the screens themselves.

Phase 1 · Entry

Screens 1, 2, 2a, 2b

Phone number and a code. Then the one decision that shapes everything, myself / a loved one, on its own screen, followed by one screen of basics. Under sixty seconds total.

Phase 2 · First conversation

Screens 3, 3b (3c in caregiver voice)

The first thing Sage asks is the anchor question: what type of cancer? Everything downstream adapts to the answer. Then the capability menu appears, already personalized, and on every launch after that, the app opens with the same menu plus "or just talk to me."

Phase 3 · Core loop

Screens 4a–4c, 7 (7b in caregiver voice)

The everyday features that need no profile: record a doctor visit, ask about a term, upload a record, keep a health log. The safety layer watches every message. Available from day one, forever.

Phase 4 · The interview

Screens 8–12

Triggered by any feature that requires knowing the patient's story: trial matching first, and anything we add later that depends on it. A pausable, resumable, plain-language interview that builds a trial-grade profile. Reports do the hard part: photos of pathology and labs replace questions no patient can answer.

Phase 5 · Profile and trials

Screens 13, 6

The profile is shown in both languages for confirmation, then matching runs. Results are framed honestly as "trials that may fit," with the final eligibility check always handed to the patient's doctor.

Why the branch is cheap

Caregiver mode is not a second set of screens. It is a voice layer: every string in the app carries template variables (patient name, pronoun, relationship, perspective), and the AI's system prompt carries the same context. Build fourteen screens once, render them in two voices.

Phase 1 · Entry

Sixty seconds to a conversation

Login is a phone number and a one-time code: no password, no email. The phone number is a deliberate architectural choice, not just a convenience: it becomes the universal key that later lets the same profile respond over SMS or WhatsApp from the number the patient already has. (Stack: Twilio Verify for OTP, Supabase for auth and profiles.)

Onboarding opens with the one question that branches the entire product, on its own uncluttered screen: Who are you here for: myself, or a loved one? (screen 2). The answer determines whose basics we collect next and the voice of every screen after.

The basics screen that follows asks four things: name, year of birth, gender, and where they live (screen 2a for patients, 2b for caregivers; the caregiver version describes the patient and additionally captures the caregiver's own name and relationship). Location is a "use my location" tap or a free-text city / postal code field, not a US ZIP field, so it works globally from day one. It is stored as lat/long plus a display name, because trial matching needs a distance radius, not a ZIP string.

Phase 2 · First conversation

The anchor question comes first

The moment the patient lands in the chat, Sage asks the single most important question: what type of cancer are you facing? This is not part of any feature; it belongs to getting to know the patient. A companion that doesn't ask it immediately feels oddly incurious. And it is the anchor fact: every downstream question (sites of spread, treatments, biomarkers worth looking for) branches on the answer.

There are hundreds of cancer types, so this cannot be a dropdown. The screen offers a handful of common chips plus a type-ahead that accepts plain words: "stomach" finds gastric cancer, "blood cancer" offers leukemia, lymphoma, and myeloma. Two honest escape hatches are always present: "We're still finding out", because many people arrive mid-workup and scared, without a name for it yet; and the caregiver path. For the still-being-diagnosed patient, the menu that follows reorders to lead with visit recording and term explanations, and trial matching waits.

After the answer, the capability menu appears, and it is already personalized. "Find clinical trials for breast cancer" signals that Sage is paying attention, and it means the trial interview later starts at question two, not question one.

Screen 3b is also the app's permanent home. Once the anchor question has been answered, every launch opens here: a short greeting, the four or five ways Sage can help, and "or just talk to me. Ask anything, anytime." The menu stays personalized to the patient's situation, and the open invitation keeps the door wide for whatever is actually on their mind that day.

Voice input everywhere

Every chat input row includes a microphone alongside the plus (upload) and send buttons. For this audience (older patients, stressed patients, tired caregivers), speaking is often easier than typing, and a later iteration should also read Sage's replies aloud as an option.

Phase 3 · Core loop

Useful from day one, no profile required

Two features carry the everyday value of Sage, and neither needs any profile at all. This matters: a patient can quit the trial interview halfway, or never start it, and still get real help every single day.

The visit recorder

The patient taps record before a doctor visit. Sage transcribes live, and when the visit ends it produces the three things patients actually want, not a transcript: what the doctor said in plain words, what I do next, and what to ask next time. The visit is saved to the health log permanently.

Consent is handled up front: a one-tap "I've told my doctor I'm recording" confirmation, with a suggested script ("Do you mind if I record this so I can review it later?"). Recording-consent laws vary by state and country, and doctors almost always say yes when asked.

The safety layer

A classifier runs on every message before the normal chat responds. When a message describes urgent symptoms (the canonical example is fever during chemotherapy, a possible neutropenic emergency), Sage does not chat. It escalates, unambiguously, with two large buttons: call the care team, call 911. Then, and only then, it offers one useful non-medical action (logging the symptom with a timestamp for the doctor) so the patient feels helped rather than dismissed. The red-flag symptom list must be authored and reviewed by a clinician, not by the engineering team.

The "Call my care team" button only works if we have the number, so within the first days of use, Sage should proactively ask "who's your oncologist, and what's the office number?" and store it. This is the one just-in-time exception worth prompting early, because it pays off exactly when the patient can't think straight.

Phase 4 · The interview

A trial-grade profile, built in plain words

Visit recording needs no profile. Trial matching needs a nearly perfect one, and it is the first of what will be several features that depend on knowing the patient's full story. Any future capability that requires that knowledge triggers the same interview, and benefits from it having been done once. There are thousands of registered trials, and for any given patient only a handful genuinely fit. The fit depends on eligibility criteria far beyond location: cancer subtype, extent of disease, sites of spread, biomarkers, number of prior lines of therapy, performance status, lab values, and disqualifying conditions.

No sixth grader knows their "stage" or their "ECOG score." So the interview never uses those words. Each clinical variable maps to a question a patient can actually answer, and Sage extrapolates the answer to correct medical vocabulary behind the scenes:

What matching needsWhat Sage asksHow it maps
Stage / extent of disease"Has the cancer spread?" No, it's in one place / To lymph nodes nearby / To other parts of my bodyLocalized · regional · metastatic
Sites of metastasis"Which parts? Tap all your doctor has mentioned." Bones, liver, lungs, brain...Brain, liver, lung involvement each open or close whole categories of trials
Prior lines of therapy"What treatments have you had?" (checklist) + "Did the cancer come back or grow during any of these?"The second question counts lines without ever saying "lines"
Performance status (ECOG)"On most days, which sounds most like you?" Four answers from "up and about, living normally" to "in bed or a chair most of the day"ECOG 0–3
Comorbidities / exclusions"Do any of these apply to you?" Heart problems, diabetes, hepatitis or HIV, autoimmune conditionCommon exclusion criteria
Biomarkers & lab valuesNot asked at all. "Take a photo of your pathology report or recent blood work."Extracted from uploads, then confirmed by the patient in plain words
Two structural rules

Every answer saves the instant it's given, so the interview is pausable and resumable with zero loss. And the interview never blocks the rest of the app: a patient can quit halfway and still record tomorrow's doctor visit.

Phase 5 · Profile and trials

Both languages, honest matches

Before matching runs, the patient sees their whole story back, in their words and in their medical team's words. This screen does three jobs at once: the patient verifies we understood them, they gently learn their own medical vocabulary, and the profile becomes a document they can literally show their oncologist. The dual-language profile is a product feature in its own right, independent of trial matching.

The matches are framed honestly. The interview gets a patient to roughly 80% match precision; the remaining 20% (specific lab thresholds, washout periods, measurable-disease criteria) cannot be self-reported reliably. So results say "trials that may fit you," descriptions are translated from researcher language into plain words on demand, and every trial card carries a "Questions for my doctor" button, because the final eligibility check is always a conversation with the care team.

Profiles go stale

A patient whose cancer progresses is exactly the one who needs fresh matches. Sage should gently re-open the interview every few months ("has anything changed since we last talked about your treatment?"), and any visit recording that mentions progression should trigger the same prompt.

The voice layer

One app, two voices

Caregiver mode is a rendering of the same fourteen screens, not a second product. Every user-facing string carries template variables (patient name, pronoun, relationship, and a self/caregiver perspective flag), and the AI's system prompt carries the same context. "What type of cancer are you facing?" becomes "What type of cancer is Rosa facing?" The safety screen becomes "Rosa needs a doctor now. Call her care team."

Two deliberate touches in the caregiver voice: Sage occasionally acknowledges the caregiver themselves ("Caring for your mom is a lot." "You're doing the right thing, Miguel."). Caregivers are exhausted and invisible, and a companion that sometimes sees them, not only the patient, is what makes people stay. And for the daily-life questions, caregivers often answer more accurately than patients do; Sage says so, which turns a data-quality quirk into a moment of respect.

The data model supports this from day one: the phone number owns the account, and the account contains a patient profile that may or may not be the account holder. This future-proofs the case where a caregiver manages a parent's profile and the parent later joins from their own phone.

Profile schema (all nullable): cancer_type, cancer_subtype, extent (localized / regional / metastatic), metastasis_sites[], biomarkers[], prior_treatments[], progressed_on_treatment, prior_lines (derived), ecog (derived), comorbidities[], labs{}, care_team{name, phone}, location{lat, lng, display}, plus account-level: account_holder_name, perspective (self / caregiver), relationship, patient_name.

Safety classifier: runs before the chat model on every inbound message. Red-flag list authored by a clinician (fever on treatment, uncontrolled bleeding, sudden confusion, chest pain, suicidal distress → warm human resources). When triggered, the escalation card renders before any conversational reply.

Content rules: sixth-grade reading level everywhere; "I'm not sure" on every question; nothing Sage says is diagnostic; extraction always confirmed by the patient before it enters the profile.
